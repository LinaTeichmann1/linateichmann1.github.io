Lina Teichmann, PhD

Postdoc in Cognitive Neuroscience at the National Institute of Mental Health

Research Interests

My research focuses broadly on visual perception and how we make sense of what we see. I am intrigued by neural representations of things that are not currently visible. My current line of research focuses on perceptual gaps caused by occlusion and eyeblinks. Our visual input is frequently interrupted by perceptual gaps and yet, our visual perception of the world seems continuous. I study how we maintain and update visual information through such gaps to achieve meaningful vision. I use brain imaging methods (MEG, fMRI), modelling, and behavioural approaches to answer my research questions.

About me

I'm originally from Aachen in Germany. After high school, I moved to Groningen in the Netherlands to study Cognitive Psychology and Psychophysiology. I spent two semesters abroad, one in Canada (Queen's University) and one in Australia (Macquarie University). After finishing my Masters thesis (supervised by Mark Nieuwenstein ), I returned to Australia to complete a combined Masters and PhD programme in Cognitive Science (supervised by Anina Rich). Today, I'm a visiting fellow at the National Institute of Mental Health working with Chris Baker in the Laboratory of Brain and Cognition.

I enjoy teaching, science communication and outreach. Occasionally, I write blog posts for Brain Post, a blog that aims to communicate new neuroscience findings to the general public. I've also given a public lecture and several interviews about synaesthesia. During my PhD, I was a teaching assistant for several undergraduate courses and recently I have started voluntering as a content reviewer and teaching assistant for Neuromatch Academy.
